Passive Voice in English Grammar

Usage Formula Examples

What is "Passive Voice" ?

Passive Voice is a grammatical construction in which the subject of a sentence is acted upon by the verb, rather than performing the action. In other words, the focus is on the receiver of the action rather than the doer (the agent).

In Passive Voice, the object of the action becomes the subject of the sentence, The verb often includes a form of "to be" (e.g., am, is, are, was ,were) combined with the past participle of the main verb.

The Usage of the twelve tenses in Passive Voice

Passive Voice tenses The Usage
1. Present Simple Passive We use "Present Simple Passive" to describe the action that happen in the present, but the focus is on the action rather than who is performing it.
2. Present Continuous Simple Passive We use "Present Continuous Simple Passive" to describe the actions that are happening right now but where the focus is on the action being done to the subject, not on who is performing the action.
3. Past Simple Passive We use "Past Simple Passive" to describe the action that was completed in the past, where the focus is on the action being done on the subject rather than who performed the action.
4. Past Continuous Simple Passive We use "Past Continuous Simple Passive" to describe the action that was in progress at a specific time in the past which focusing on the action being done to the subject rather than who performed it.
5. Future Simple Passive We use "Future Simple Passive" to describe about the action that will be done to the subject in the future.
6. Future Continuous Simple Passive We use "Future Continuous Simple Passive" to describe the action that will be ongoing in a specific time in the future, with the focus on the action being done to the subject.
7. Present Perfect Passive We use "Present Perfect Passive" to describe the action that have been completed at some point in the past but is relevant to the present. The focus is on the action being done to the subject, rather than who performed the action.
8. Present Continuous Perfect Passive We use "Present Continuous Perfect Passive" to describe tha action that started in the past, has been happening for some time and is still ongoing, but where the emphasis is on the action being done to the subject (but it is not commonly used).
9. Past Perfect Passive We use "Past Perfect Passive" to describe the action that was completed before another action in the past at a specific point, focusing on the action being done to the subject rather than who performed the action.
10. Past Continuous Perfect Passive We use "Past Continuous Perfect Passive" to describe the action that had been ongoing in the past and was being done to the subject.
11. Future Perfect Passive We use "Future Perfect Passive" to describe the action that will have been completed by a specific point in the future, focusing on the action being done to the subject rather than who performed it.
12. Future Continuous Perfect Passive We use "Future Continuous Perfect Passive" to describe the action that have been ongoing up to a specific point in the future, focusing on the action being done to the subject.

    The Easy-Steps to help you understand well about the Passive Voice

    Understanding the "Passive Voice" becomes easier when you break it down into steps :

    1. Identify the Active Sentence Structure

    Active sentences follow this pattern : Subject + Verb + Object

  • Example : "The teacher (subject) explains (verb) the lesson (object)."
    •

    2. Switch to Passive Structure

    In Passive Voice, the object of the Active sentence becomes the subject of the Passive sentence. The focus is on who or what is being acted upon.

  • Passive Structure : Subject + Be (in correct tense) + Verb(3) + By + Agent
  • Example : "The lesson (subject) is (be) explained (verb3) by the teacher (by + agent)."
    •

    3. Use the correct tense of the Verb "to be"

    The form of "to be" changes depending on the tense of the original sentence.

  • Example (Present) : "The lesson is explained."
  • Example (Past) : " The lesson was explained."
  • Example (Future) : "The lesson will be explained."
    •

    4. Optional : Mention the Agent (Who is doing the action)

    In Passive Voice, the agent is often left out if it is not important or obvious.

  • Example : "The lession is explained by the teacher." You can omit "by the teacher" if it is clear from the context.
    •

    5. Practice Converting Active Voice to Passive Voice

    It is one of the best way to make us clear about the Passive Voice. For Example :

  • Active Voice tense : The chef cooked the meal.
  • Passive Voice tense : The meal was cooked by the chef.
    •

    The Benefits that you gain from learning Passive Voice

    Learning and mastering the Passive Voice offers several benefits, especailly in communication and writing. Here are some key advantages :

    1. Focus on the action or result

    Passive Voice empasizes what happened or the action itself, rather than who performed it.

  • Example : "The project was completed" focuses on the completion, which may be more important than who did it.

    • 2. Neutral and Objective tone

    It helps in maintaining a more nuetral tone, often used in formal writing like academic, scientific, and news reports to avoid pointing fingers.

  • Example : "Mistakes were made." Rather than "You made mistakes."

    • 3. Useful when the agent is unknown or irrelevant

    In some cases, who performed the action is either unknown or unimportant.

  • Example : "The window was broken." Rather than "We don't know who broke it, but the broken window is what matters."

    • 4. Politeness and tact

    Passive Voice can be a tool for being more polite or diplomatic by avoiding direct blame.

  • Example : "Your application was not approved." Sound more polite than "We did not approve your application."

    • 5. Consistency in writing

    In research, technical, or formal writing, using passive voice ensures consistency, especially when describing processes or results.

  • Example : "The experiment was conducted, and data eas analyzed."

    • 6.Enhances clarity and variety in writing

    In complex sentences, passive voice can improve clarity by simplifying the sentence structure and varying the sentence patterns.

  • Example : "The song was written in 2005." Clearer than "Someone wrote the song in 2005."

    • Understanding and applying passive voice allows for greater flexibility and precision in communication, depending on the context and what you want to emphasize.
